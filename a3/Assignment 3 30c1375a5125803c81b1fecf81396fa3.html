Critique

The visualization focuses on one takeaway: the number of cases of Omicron over three years. The visual encodings used are area and curvature, which are low on the effectiveness ranks. It does not use any color or different channels but it does use spatial region to signify the month. It uses a spiral shape to express multiple years of data, which I think is my main critique. The reader has to start at the center and follow a spiral, comparing the thickness which is being reoriented. It has the same negative effect as writing the column labels sideways causing the reader to need to tilt their head. The legend indicates that the a certain area is 150k, but it is really hard to look at the plot and estimate what the magnitude because the datapoints are all oriented differently compared to the legend (except for in 3 locations where it aligns). The labels used are the year, month, and an annotation that explains that they use a 7-day average to plot. Something that is confusing is the data lines are very smooth, despite using a 7-day average. Since there are no datapoints or indicators, I’m not sure if the line is representing a 7-day moving average, or just a 7-day average and they apply smoothing between each week.

I think the target audience will not easily understand the plot. This is the first time looking at a spiral plot, and it took me time to understand where to start looking, how time increases in a circle, then how area changes over a curve. It does look like the cases for Jan 2022 at the time are growing the steepest, so I can tell that the author wants to convey that the cases are growing more than it ever has since the start of the pandemic. I would say this conclusion using area encoding is not the easiest to make compared to encodings in position or length. Overall, I think this visualization is creative. It tries to focus on one trend and it does not overwhelm the reader with information, but I think it is not as effective as it could be and likely will be too difficult for the average reader to read. Especially since this is an article meant for the mass public, I think the visualization could do a better job at making the visualization more intuitive perceptually.

Final Visualization

Reflection Writeup

The takeaway from the original visualization was that at the time (Jan 2022) the number of new cases were at the highest. I wanted to keep this as my message of my visualization as well. I built on my last sketch with a length encoding. I used a moving average (similar as the original visualization) and used the length of each bar to represent that amount. I mirrored the length across an invisible vertical axis in order to make the plot symmetric. In my last sketch, I mentioned using circles to fill in the bars instead of solid coloring. I thought that this would give more texture and make the plot more interesting to look at, but when I tried it out it obscured the trends rather than helped make it readable. I also experimented with different styles for the bars. At first they were sharp rectangles, but I since the data was a moving average which is smooth by construction, I also wanted the whole plot to have smooth features so I used rounded bars instead of default bars. Instead of averaging over a week, I increased the length of days averaged over to make the plot shorter in height. This smooths over some of the plateaus that are less noticeable now. This is a trade off of expressing more of the data versus prioritizing the trend. In terms of annotations and labeling, I wanted to simplify the message. I included direct annotations for the data around the year 2021 (198k) in order to provide numeric context and contrast for the datapoint at the start of 2022 (615k). Instead of just labeling 615k, I labeled it as “all time high” in red in order to emphasize the message. Another subtle design choice I spent time on was coloring. I chose the color of the plot to be a sort of murky and swamp color to match the “snot” aesthetic I wanted to achieve. I also chose the background as off-white to make the graphic softer on the eyes compared to an all white background.

I experimented with different titles across all the sketches. I decided on the final title by stating the message I want the reader to understand directly, “stay home”. I iterated on the description of the trend and wanted to highlight that the recent uptick is not just a gradual “rise” or increase but it is indeed a spike.

I was not able to address all of the critiques I had in step 2. My main critique was that the spiral shape was not familiar and readable to the reader. In my final plot, I used an inverted axis and vertical plot, which is an improvement compared to a spiral, but can still be confusing to read. I tried to support this design choice by adding direct annotations and simplifying my presentation of the message. In making this final figure, I worked through a tension between capturing attention and having distraction. I included the nose depiction and made the visualization to illicit an “ew” emotional response to draw the reader in, but I did not want the nose depiction to throw off the reader too much. I played around with the size of the nose to make it take up less space in the graphic, and I ended up with approximately using a third of the space for the nose. More than the nose illustration, the size of the font, the annotations, and even arrows, all mattered and contributed to how the visualization is read. I tried to choose the size of these elements with their importance: title larger than year which is larger than annotations. Another critique I had for the original visualization was that the “7-day” annotation was not informative and the legend was also hard to use. I tried to solve this in my final visualization by not including what the average is over in order to focus the reader on the relative trends based more on the year than week/month. I also did not use a legend, but instead labeled the middle and last bar with their magnitudes. In doing this, I’m including less information, but I believe this helps streamline the message to the reader for the context of this visualization. Overall, I had fun making this visualization. Compared to previous sketches, I pushed myself to try something creative, and in doing so I encountered new design decisions and tradeoffs than I originally thought I would have.
