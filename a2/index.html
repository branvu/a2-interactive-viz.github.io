Residential Sales Data

I’m interested in understanding the dataset about Boston residential sales from 2000-2023. It has 49301 rows and 63 different attributes including address, type of property, price, date, investor types, and others. I noticed the following data quality issues.

MBTA Network

This dataset consists of geographical data about the MBTA stops, lines and types of transit that exists in the greater Boston area.

Analysis Questions

1. Do sale prices differ by distance to the nearest MBTA stop (or line)?

Motivation: Based on TOD https://mbtarealty.com/transit-oriented-development/ , I want to know how effective these programs are by seeing if affordable housing is being developed near transit.

2. Among properties built in recent decades (>2000), is new construction more concentrated near transit?

Motivation: Compared to all housing, I want to know how are newer housing projects are related to sales. For young people moving to Boston, it’s important to know how newer Boston housing is developing. Determining where these newer housing is being built and sold is important for future home-buyers. Also, according to https://www.worldbank.org/en/topic/transport/publication/transforming-the-urban-space-through-transit-oriented-development-the-3v-approach, it’s important to assess the potential market value for considering development for TOD. For new properties, we should determine how well existing policies have worked to enable the buying and selling of properties near transit.

A followup question that I’m curious of, is how has the price changed over time of new housing. Is the newer housing getting more expensive compared to all housing? We would expect some increase from inflation, but based on the areas where most of the new housing is located, perhaps the housing is affordable.

3. Where are lower-price sales (e.g. bottom quartile) relative to transit?

Motivation: I want to know whether lower-priced housing is close or far to transit, or most of the housing being sold near transit is expensive. As a future home-owner, I want to know if housing near transit is affordable or something that is mostly for the highest-paying buyers.

Another follow-up question related to affordability, is how many of these sale transactions are cash-based. Most individual people rely on mortgages to purchase housing, but investors or corporations can out-bid individuals by offering cash and buying properties as investments.

Summary

For new home-buyers in Boston, it’s important to understand where housing is built and sold, as well as trends with who is buying properties and what properties types are being sold. I first focused on the cost of housing and whether there is a relationship between location and purchase price. There were clearly patterns partially related to proximity to transit along green and orange subway lines. Next, I wanted to investigate what the trends were with housing built in 2000s and on. I found that they were mostly in the South End of Boston and were dominated by units sold from mid to high rise apartments. As a lower bound, even the cheapest housing units were still close to MBTA stops which shows that MBTA has good coverage (mostly bus stops) regardless of housing price. Lastly, I wanted to see if cash purchases were on the rise, and it looks like although they increased since 2005, they are plateauing. All of these factors contribute to determining how transit and affordable housing are related based on spatial and housing purchase transaction data. To further understand affordable housing, in the future we should also look at the housing stock, not just buying and selling transactions. This would give us a sense of where people are currently living versus where people have bought and sold.
